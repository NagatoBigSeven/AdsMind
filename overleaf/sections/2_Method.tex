The three closed-loop mechanisms described in Section~\ref{sec:intro}---Chemical slip detection, FORBID constraints, and convergence-based early stopping---are implemented across five specialized modules: \textbf{Planner}, \textbf{Validator}, \textbf{Executor}, \textbf{Analyzer}, and \textbf{Summarizer}. We now detail their implementation.

\subsection{Closed-Loop Agent Architecture}

\subsubsection{Planner and Validator: Adsorption Hypothesis Generation}

With a request prompt provided by the user, which consists of adsorbate SMILES string, and surface slab file, the \textbf{Planner} proposes a structured adsorption hypothesis in JSON format at each iteration, including the canonical site type (\textit{ontop}, \textit{bridge}, or \textit{hollow}), intended surface symbols, and adsorbate binding indices~\cite{norskov2011density}.

In this close-loop architecture, the prompt input into the \textbf{Planner} is updated from other modules as physical feedback, and assembled from conditional sections controlled by three runtime switches:
\textit{Slip feedback} frames prior slips as evidence that the planned site is unstable;
\textit{Forbid} converts slipped or failed site intents into explicit prohibitions;
\textit{Termination} surfaces convergence-to-known-best signals and allows early stopping.
The three switches are independent, and disabling all three, together with a single-attempt budget, yields the 1-Shot baseline.

After the hypothesis is proposed, the \textbf{Validator} enforces chemistry- and schema-level constraints before any expensive physics simulation:
(1)~requested adsorbate binding indices are valid and correspond to non-hydrogen heavy atoms (unless the adsorbate is a single atom);
(2)~cardinality of binding indices matches the site type (e.g., \textit{ontop} requires exactly one index);
(3)~the hypothesis has not been previously evaluated, preventing redundant calculations.
Failed hypotheses elicit an immediate penalty feedback, directing the LLM to refine its proposal without advancing to physical simulation.

\subsubsection{Executor and Analyzer: Physical Simulation and Scientific Analysis}

The \textbf{Executor} instantiates the validated plan on the slab, performs physical relaxation, and returns structured post-relaxation analysis.
The \textbf{Analyzer} records whether the final structure remained bound, whether dissociation or rearrangement occurred, and whether the realized coordination environment differs from the \textbf{Planner}'s intent.
\textit{Chemical slip} is detected when the relaxed structure exhibits a different surface-symbol fingerprint (sorted element symbols within the first coordination shell of the anchor atom) than the \textbf{Planner}'s intent; canonical site-family mismatch is retained separately as a geometric slip signal.

Before entered into the \textbf{Executor}, the proposal is pre-processed through a surrogate-SMILES geometry layer that maps binding atoms to proxy markers and constructs initial placements for \textit{ontop}, \textit{bridge}, and \textit{hollow} adsorption.
It samples a capped set of candidate surface sites and conformers (default: eight filtered coordinates, four conformers per site; one top-ranked candidate proceeds to full relaxation).

Then a \textbf{two-step evaluation procedure} is employed: candidates are first screened with the MACE-MP calculator to discard pathological configurations and rank by screened energy (Langevin warmup disabled in primary benchmark); the best surviving candidate is then fully relaxed and analyzed.

The adsorption energy is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{ads}} = E_{\text{total}} - E_{\text{surface}} - E_{\text{adsorbate}}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{total}}$ is the relaxed adsorbate-slab energy, $E_{\text{surface}}$ is the bare surface single-point energy, and $E_{\text{adsorbate}}$ is the isolated adsorbate energy.

Relaxation uses the BFGS algorithm~\cite{nocedal2006numerical} with a maximum force convergence of $f_{\text{max}} = 0.10$~eV/\AA\ and a cap of 200 steps (primary benchmark parameters; see SI Table~S1 for full settings). The bottom third of slab atoms are fixed along the $z$-axis; upper layers and adsorbate atoms are fully relaxed.

The \textbf{Analyzer} then parses the relaxed trajectory to extract the local coordination environment.
Connectivity is established via an adjacency matrix $A$: atoms $i$ and $j$ are bonded if $d_{ij} \le \gamma(R_i + R_j)$, with $\gamma=1.35$ for intramolecular dissociation checks and $\gamma=1.30$ (relaxed to $1.45$ for $E_{\text{ads}} < -0.5$~eV) for adsorbate--surface coordination.
Dissociation is flagged if the adsorbate subgraph has $>1$ connected component; the coordination environment classifies the site as \textit{ontop}, \textit{bridge}, or \textit{hollow}.
Slip is logged if the realized site or surface-symbol fingerprint differs from the \textbf{Planner}'s intent.
The \textbf{Analyzer} updates running memory with adsorption energy, bond integrity, and slip status, enabling dynamic adjustment of the exploration strategy.

\subsubsection{Summarizer: Convergence Monitoring, Reporting, and Early Stopping}

The \textbf{Summarizer} consolidates the running memory maintained by the \textbf{Analyzer} across iterations.
Convergence is declared when the same site family is proposed in three consecutive iterations without finding a lower-energy configuration.
Upon convergence, the \textbf{Summarizer} emits a STOP signal to terminate the search early, preventing unnecessary computation when further exploration is unlikely to improve the result.

At termination, the \textbf{Summarizer} writes a deterministic Markdown report, documenting the run context (surface, adsorbate, SMILES), the best adsorption energy and configuration, a per-iteration table recording planned vs.\ actual binding sites, slip events, and dissociation flags, the selected \textbf{Planner} output, and a structured analysis of the final result.
The report also includes two rendered visualizations: a ball-and-stick image of the best adsorption configuration and an iteration-by-iteration energy convergence curve .
These artifacts are generated deterministically outside the LLM reasoning loop; visualization failures are recorded in the report as notes rather than raised as errors, so they never affect the scientific search outcome.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol and Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval_setup}

We evaluate five variants in a systematic ablation: \textbf{Full} (all mechanisms enabled); \textbf{w/o Slip} (removes slip feedback and FORBID constraints); \textbf{w/o Forbid} (retains slip detection but suppresses prohibition); \textbf{w/o Term} (disables early stopping); and \textbf{1-Shot} (single attempt, all mechanisms disabled).

All benchmark runs use frozen experiment configurations. Four reasoning backends are evaluated at zero temperature: Gemini~2.5~Pro~\cite{gemini2026modelcard}, GPT-5.4~\cite{openai2026gpt5}, Claude~Sonnet~4.6~\cite{anthropic2026claude}, and Grok-4~\cite{grok2025blog} (see SI Table~S1 for model identifiers).

\subsection{Physical Execution}
% \subsection{Physical Backend: MACE-MP-0 Calculator}
\label{sec:mace_config}

The physical engine uses the MACE equivariant message-passing architecture with the MACE-MP foundation model~\cite{batatia2022mace,batatia2023foundation}.
Two configurations are employed:
\textbf{Primary benchmark}: MACE-MP-0 small, CPU, float32, dispersion disabled (conservative setting held fixed across all ablations).
\textbf{Sensitivity checks}: MACE-MP-0 large, CUDA, float64, dispersion enabled (probes force-field sensitivity separately).
All primary benchmark and matched-baseline experiments use the same MACE-MP-0 small backend so that observed differences reflect search strategy, not energy-function choice. Key parameters are provided in the Supporting Information (Table~S1). To validate the physical fidelity of these MLFF-based results, we performed complementary DFT reference calculations on a representative subset of systems (Section~\ref{sec:dft_reference}).

% \subsection{DFT Reference Calculations}
\label{sec:dft_reference}

To validate the physical fidelity of MLFF-relaxed configurations against an established ground truth, we performed density functional theory (DFT) reference calculations on six representative CMU20 systems, selected to span three surface compositions---Mo$_3$Pd(111), CuPd$_3$(111), and Pt(111)/(100)---and three adsorbates (H, NNH, OH; specific surface--adsorbate pairings are listed in the Supporting Information, Table~S2). These systems cover both the simple and structurally complex adsorbates where MLFF-based search strategies exhibit the largest quantitative deviations from DFT.

All DFT calculations used the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)~6.4.2~\cite{kresse1996efficient,kresse1999ultrasoft} with PBE exchange-correlation functional~\cite{perdew1996generalized} and PAW pseudopotentials. Input files were generated with VASPKIT~1.5.1~\cite{wang2021vaspkit}.

Atomic models were constructed with Materials Studio 2023. Slab models consisted of four atomic layers expanded to $2\times2$ supercells with 15~\AA\ vacuum perpendicular to the surface; the bottom two layers were fixed to mimic the underlying bulk. A plane-wave cutoff of 400~eV was used for pristine slabs, increased to 520~eV for adsorbate-containing systems due to the presence of hydrogen.

Spin polarization was enabled (ISPIN~=~2) for all adsorbate calculations, with initial magnetic moments set as follows: H adsorption---1~$\mu_\mathrm{B}$ on the H atom, 0 on all substrate atoms; OH adsorption---1~$\mu_\mathrm{B}$ on the O atom only; NNH adsorption---1~$\mu_\mathrm{B}$ on the terminal N atom only. These settings were retained in the subsequent self-consistent field (SCF) calculations.

Structural relaxations were performed for both pristine slabs and adsorbate-covered slabs (the latter constructed from fully relaxed pristine slabs). The Methfessel--Paxton smearing scheme~\cite{methfessel1989high} (ISMEAR~=~1, $\sigma=0.2$~eV) was applied, with Monkhorst--Pack $k$-point sampling~\cite{monkhorst1976special} at 0.03~\AA$^{-1}$ for relaxation and $\Gamma$-centered 0.02~\AA$^{-1}$ for SCF calculations; $k$-point meshes were generated with VASPKIT. The maximum numbers of ionic and electronic steps were both set to 200 (ALGO~=~Normal), and the electronic convergence threshold during relaxation was $10^{-5}$~eV. After relaxation, the WAVECAR and CHGCAR files were used to initialize the SCF calculations, in which the electronic convergence criterion was tightened to $10^{-7}$~eV while all other parameters (cutoff energy, spin polarization, ISMEAR, and ALGO) were kept identical to those employed during relaxation.

% \subsection{Non-LLM Baselines}
\label{sec:nonllm_baselines}

To isolate the contribution of the LLM planner from that of the underlying MACE-MP relaxation pipeline, we implement a non-LLM heuristic control using the same MACE-MP-0 small force field (FF). The \textit{heuristic enumeration} baseline identifies all high-symmetry sites via Autoadsorbate~\cite{fako2025simple} (median 56 sites per case) and relaxes each under the same protocol (BFGS, $f_\mathrm{max}=0.10$~eV/\AA, 200 steps). This baseline operates in an open-loop regime without the chemical-safety filters (dissociation detection, connectivity audit) that AdsMind applies to its own summaries; this design choice preserves the baseline as a faithful representative of conventional enumeration strategies, while the absence of filtering is explained in the results interpretation.

% \subsection{Adsorb-Agent Comparison Protocol}
\label{sec:adsorbagent_comparison}

To isolate the effect of search strategy from the choice of force field, we forked the open-source Adsorb-Agent codebase~\cite{adsorbagent} and replaced its EquiformerV2~\cite{liao2024equiformerv2} relaxation backend with the same MACE-MP-0 small (CPU, float32, dispersion disabled) calculator used for the primary benchmark. All remaining protocol parameters were locked to AdsMind's settings: BFGS optimizer, $f_\mathrm{max} = 0.10$~eV/\AA, 200 steps, and identical \textsc{FixAtoms} constraints. We ran the modified Adsorb-Agent with GPT-5.4 on both the CMU20 and OCD-GMAE62 benchmarks.
