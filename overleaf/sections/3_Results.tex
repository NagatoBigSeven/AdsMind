\subsection{DFT Verification and Comparison}

To benchmark the physical fidelity of adsorption energies predicted by the LLM-driven search strategies against an established ground truth, we compared AdsMind and the original Adsorb-Agent results calculated with EquiformerV2 FF from Ref.~\citenum{ock2026adsorbagent} against DFT reference calculations (VASP/PBE, Section~\ref{sec:dft_reference}) on six representative CMU20 systems (cases~1, 2, 3, 4, 9, and~10). These systems cover three surface compositions (Mo$_3$Pd(111), CuPd$_3$(111), Pt(111)/(100)) and three adsorbates (H, NNH, OH), which were selected for their high practical relevance in real-world applications:
Mo$_3$Pd and CuPd$_3$ are both experimentally established catalysts for both hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR)~\cite{zhou2023enhanced}, while Pt serves as the benchmark noble-metal catalyst for HER and oxygen evolution reaction (OER)~\cite{cheng2016platinum}. Results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vasp_validation}.

Figure~\ref{fig:vasp_validation} compares the two LLM-driven strategies with the reference PBE calculation.
Adsorb-Agent exhibits large systematic deviations from PBE calculations, with a mean absolute error (MAE) of 1.84~eV and a maximum deviation exceeding 3.5~eV on Pt(111)--OH, while AdsMind exhibits an MAE of 1.55~eV.
The open-loop method produces qualitative sign errors on structurally complex molecular adsorbates. It predicts endothermic binding for OH on both Pt(111) and Pt(100) ($+0.99$~eV for both), while PBE calculations yield strongly exothermic values ($-2.59$~eV and $-2.51$~eV, respectively). Similarly, NNH on CuPd$_3$(111) is predicted as endothermic in Adsorb-Agent ($+0.745$~eV vs.\ PBE $-0.996$~eV).
These sign errors would incorrectly eliminate Pt and CuPd$_3$ as unreasonable candidate catalysts (false negative results).
For simple adsorbates such as atomic H, Adsorb-Agent achieves reasonable accuracy (maximum error $\le 0.36$~eV), but structurally complex molecular adsorbates in this validation subset drive its MAE to 1.84~eV with a high rate of sign errors.

AdsMind achieves better agreement with PBE calculations (MAE 1.55~eV) and 100\% correctness in predicting the sign of adsorption energy across all six systems.
Although AdsMind quantitatively overbinds certain Mo$_3$Pd configurations, which is consistent with the known tendency of MACE-MP-0 on early-transition-metal alloys~\cite{casillas2024evaluating}, it correctly identifies exothermic binding in each case, including the Pt--OH and CuPd$_3$--NNH systems where the open-loop method fails.
The quantitative MAE of 1.55~eV is partly attributable to the MACE-MP-0 small force field; a sensitivity analysis with MACE-MP-0 large shows mean absolute shifts exceeding 1~eV across the full CMU20 benchmark (Supporting Information).
No sign errors occur for simple H-on-metal systems under either architecture, implying that sign-correctness failures in open-loop search are likely contingent on adsorbate identity and binding complexity.
This comparison exposes a critical qualitative failure in open-loop search, namely, sign errors on molecular adsorbates, that the closed-loop architecture systematically prevents.

AdsMind converges on sign-correct configurations for all six systems, while Adsorb-Agent produces sign errors on 3 of the 4 non-H systems.
Such sign errors have direct effects on computational screening. Predicting an endothermic binding when the true interaction is strongly exothermic fundamentally misrepresents the surface chemistry and leads to negative elimination of viable catalyst candidates.
AdsMind reduces failures and ensures robust reliability through closed-loop physical feedback.

\paragraph {Baselines' comparison and the breadth--depth trade-off.}
On the 20-case CMU benchmark, the heuristic enumeration (median 56 sites, range 25--98) identifies lower raw adsorption energies than AdsMind by exploring more of the same potential energy surface of MACE-MP-0 small model. This non-LLM method outperforms AdsMind in 8/20 cases, but a connectivity audit reveals that these raw gains are often chemically unstable: the top structures in 25\% (5/20) of cases exhibit fragmentation or unresolved anomalies, including NNH dissociation (cases~04, 08) and large-adsorbate fragmentation (cases~16, 19). When filtered for chemical validity, the heuristic advantage persists in only 3/20 cases.

These results delineate a fundamental trade-off in automated adsorption discovery. Broader open-loop enumeration can identify deeper minima but requires a much larger MLFF relaxation budget (heuristic enumeration: median 56 relaxations, range 25--98, versus 4.11 relaxations per case for AdsMind Full on CMU20) and produces chemically unreasonable outcomes in a non-negligible fraction of cases. AdsMind occupies the reliability-first side of this trade-off: chemically validated convergence achieved by integrating dissociation detection and iterative physical feedback, while prioritizing reliability and safety over exhaustive depth optimization.


\subsection{Ablation on the CMU20 benchmark}

We further evaluated the agent on the completed 20-case CMU benchmark, covering H, NNH, OH, CH$_2$CH$_2$OH, OCHCH$_3$, and ONN(CH$_3$)$_2$ adsorption across difficult surfaces in Figure \ref{fig:cmu20_ablation}b.
For each case, we additionally executed four ablation variants with a fixed MACE-MP-0 small FF model~\cite{batatia2023foundation}: \textit{1-Shot, without (w/o) Slip, w/o Forbid, and w/o Term} (defined in Section~\ref{sec:eval_setup}).
The complete 20$\times$5 matrix was executed independently on four LLM backend, i.e., Gemini~2.5~Pro, GPT-5.4, Claude~Sonnet~4.6, and Grok-4, yielding 393 successful runs out of 400 attempts.


Across the five ablation variants on the CMU20 benchmark, the \textit{1-Shot} configuration shows the largest deviation from \textit{Full}. 
The \textit{w/o Slip} and \textit{w/o Forbid} variants yield adsorption energies nearly identical to \textit{Full}, with differences attributable to per-case fluctuation rather than systematic bias.
\textit{w/o Term} occasionally reaches marginally lower energies than \textit{Full}, reflecting the expected trade-off where a relaxed stopping criterion permits deeper exploration with a larger relaxation budget. Overall, the \textit{Full} configuration balances reliability with controlled exploration.

% When the full closed-loop feedback is active, iterative recovery eliminates this fragility, and the four-backend analysis reveals a more consistent cross-model behavior.
Under the \textit{Full} configuration, the closed-loop architecture drives heterogeneous LLM backends toward consistent physical solutions.
To quantify this agreement, we define the four-backend range as the max-min energy spread for a given case.
Across CMU20, the mean range is 0.195~eV (median 0.037~eV), with 7/20 cases agreeing within 0.01~eV and 11/20 within 0.05~eV.
The largest disagreement occurs on case~08 (Ru$_3$Mo + NNH), where GPT-5.4 reaches $-7.191$~eV, Claude reaches $-6.869$~eV, and Gemini/Grok-4 remain at $-6.414$~eV.
Thus, AdsMind is reliable across different LLM choices, while backend agreement remains case-dependent rather than universal.

Measuring against this baseline, the \textit{1-Shot} variant---with all feedback mechanisms disabled---quantifies the value of iterative correction.
Full consistently outperforms 1-Shot across all four backends: the mean per-case improvement ranges from 0.108~eV (GPT-5.4) to 0.297~eV (Grok-4), with Gemini at 0.296~eV and Claude at 0.163~eV.
No backend shows a mean degradation, confirming that iterative feedback provides directionally consistent benefit regardless of the underlying LLM.
The variation in gain magnitude reflects each backend's one-shot planning capability: GPT-5.4, the strongest single-pass planner, exhibits the smallest mean improvement, which is consistent with less room for iterative recovery, yet still gains 0.426~eV on case~02 and 0.549~eV on case~17.
At the opposite end, Gemini and Grok-4 show the largest mean gains, concentrated on structurally complex surfaces: case~20 exhibits 1-Shot deficits exceeding 1.0~eV on both backends, case~15 degrades under 1-Shot across all four backends, and case~12 degrades on three of four (tied on GPT-5.4).
This pattern---directionally universal gain whose magnitude is modulated by backend capability and surface complexity---is the expected signature of an error-correction mechanism: the closed loop corrects mistakes whose baseline frequency depends on both the planner's strength and the adsorption problem's difficulty.

The remaining three variants---\textit{w/o Slip}, \textit{w/o Forbid}, and \textit{w/o Term}---reveal the contribution of each individual feedback mechanism.
\textit{w/o Slip} and \textit{w/o Forbid} yield adsorption energies nearly identical to \textit{Full}, with differences attributable to per-case fluctuation rather than systematic bias, suggesting partial redundancy among the three closed-loop channels.
\textit{w/o Term} occasionally reaches marginally lower energies than \textit{Full} (e.g., Grok-4 mean $-0.047$~eV), reflecting the expected trade-off where a relaxed stopping criterion permits deeper exploration with additional MLFF relaxations.
Case-level analysis reveals that the value of each mechanism is conditional: on edge cases where an early attempt happens to land in a favorable basin (e.g., case~18: \textit{w/o Slip} $-0.491$~eV vs \textit{Full} on GPT-5.4), the FORBID constraint can over-prune the remaining search space, preventing exploration of potentially better solutions.
This is a predictable trade-off: guardrails improve reliability and cross-backend consistency across the benchmark but can over-constrain cases where the initial trajectory is already favorable; \textit{w/o Forbid} and \textit{w/o Term} both trend toward improving over \textit{Full} on GPT-5.4, yet \textit{Full} maintains higher success rates and tighter backend convergence overall.

Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_ablation}c summarizes these findings in a multi-objective framework.
The radar chart compares \textit{Full} and ablation variants across success rate, energy accuracy, backend robustness, and cross-LLM agreement.
\textit{Full} provides the most balanced operating point: 100\% success rate and improved cross-backend consistency compared with other variants.
The chart does not imply that \textit{Full} is strictly optimal in raw energy depth---\textit{w/o Term} can find lower energies beyond the default termination budget---but supports the conclusion that the complete closed-loop design delivers the strongest reliability--relaxation-budget--robustness operating point.

\paragraph{Understanding closed-loop behavior across surfaces.}
Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_convergence} provides deeper insight into how the closed-loop mechanisms produce the observed ablation results by examining system behavior at three levels.

Chemical slip, where the adsorbate relaxes to a different site than the LLM intended, provides insight into the difficulty of heterogeneous surface prediction.
Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_convergence}b shows one-shot slip rates stratified by surface family.
Slip is frequent across both families: intermetallic surfaces show 61.5--76.9\% slip across backends, while monometallic surfaces show 40.0--80.0\%, indicating that surface family alone does not determine whether the LLM-planned site survives relaxation.
The high slip frequency reflects the geometric sensitivity of adsorption placement, where asymmetric local coordination and backend-specific planning choices create opportunities for structural relaxation to diverge from the planned configuration.
The systematic failure of 1-Shot on intermetallic cases (case~06, 08) in the ablation thus originates from a structural property of the surface---not from outlying LLM behavior---and the closed-loop architecture addresses it by feeding the detected mismatch back into the Planner's context.
Beyond this corrective role, slip detection provides an interpretable diagnostic signal available only in a closed-loop system: without inspecting the relaxed structure, an open-loop agent would be blind to the gap between its planned and realized configurations.


Iteration convergence (Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_convergence}a) shows how quickly the closed loop closes this gap. After excluding dissociated attempts from running-best updates, iteration~2 captures approximately 43\% of the initial gap to the final valid adsorption energy, after which returns diminish across all four backends (Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_convergence}a). Most closed-loop value is extracted within 2--3 iterations, supporting the default five-attempt budget and explaining the low MLFF relaxation budget (4.11 relaxations per case on CMU20).
Across the four backends, Gemini and GPT-5.4 show the fastest early convergence after dissociation filtering, while backend differences become modest by iteration~3.

The four-backend heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:cmu20_convergence}c) visualizes the cross-backend agreement quantified in the previous section, confirming that the iterative architecture drives heterogeneous LLM planners toward consistent physical solutions (2.42$\times$ variance reduction relative to 1-Shot, Table~\ref{tab:key-evaluation-metrics}).


\subsection{Generalization to the OCD-GMAE62 dataset}

To test whether the CMU20 conclusions generalize beyond monometallic and intermetallic surfaces, we constructed \textbf{OCD-GMAE62}, a 62-case dataset spanning oxides (e.g., Hf$_{16}$Zn$_{48}$), chalcogenides (e.g., Mo$_{18}$S$_{72}$W$_{18}$), and multi-component alloys (e.g., Cd$_{24}$Pd$_{24}$, As$_{48}$Hf$_{48}$Ni$_{48}$), with adsorbates from single atoms (H, N) to complex species (CH$_2$CH$_2$OH, ONN(CH$_3$)$_2$).
OCD-GMAE62 is designed as a boundary probe, not a larger CMU20 to reveal where closed-loop reasoning succeeds on diverse chemistry and where it does not.
We evaluate it through a two-tier protocol: Tier~1 runs the complete five-variant ablation matrix (Full, w/o Slip, w/o Forbid, w/o Term, 1-Shot) across all 62 cases and four LLM backends, yielding 1240 attempts (Table~\ref{tab:ocd62-tier1-full}); Tier~2 repeats 12 cases with $N=3$ independent runs to quantify run-to-run stochasticity.

\subsubsection{Tier~1: Full-matrix evaluation}

Full achieves 98.8\% success on Tier~1 (245/248), while 1-Shot drops to 89.5\% (222/248) (Figure~\ref{fig:ocd62-two-tier}a).
The three Full failures are concentrated on case~053 (K$_{20}$ + C([CH$_2$])O), where GPT-5.4, Gemini, and Grok-4 undergo natural adsorbate dissociation; Claude succeeds, confirming that the failure is a physical chemical event rather than a framework crash---the dissociating backends correctly identify surface instability, while Claude finds a bound configuration through an alternative site hypothesis.
All 33 failures across the ablation matrix are natural chemistry outcomes, with no API or implementation errors.

The aggregate 1-Shot penalty confirms that closed-loop iteration improves energy depth: mean $\Delta E = E_{\mathrm{1-Shot}} - E_{\mathrm{Full}} = +0.329$~eV (median 0.067~eV across 222 paired successful cases), larger than the $+0.222$~eV penalty on CMU20 (Figure~\ref{fig:ocd62-two-tier}b).
However, this mean conceals extreme case-level heterogeneity that defines when closed-loop reasoning matters.
On complex, multi-element surfaces where no single high-symmetry site dominates, iterative feedback provides large gains: case~004 (Pt$_{16}$V$_{48}$ + [N]=O) improves by 1.05~eV, case~005 (As$_{48}$Hf$_{48}$Ni$_{48}$ + [NH$_2$]) by 0.82~eV, case~021 by 0.79~eV---precisely the regime where CMU20 one-shot slip is common across backends.
On surfaces with a single dominant binding motif, iteration adds nothing: cases~007, 009, and 050 yield identical energies for Full and 1-Shot.
Conversely, on case~008 (Ga$_{12}$Pd$_{36}$ + [NH]), Full ($-7.48$~eV) is 0.99~eV \emph{worse} than 1-Shot ($-8.47$~eV): the LLM's first hypothesis is near-optimal, but the \texttt{FORBID} guardrail over-prunes the basin, trapping subsequent iterations in a shallower minimum.
The closed-loop advantage is thus conditional---largest when first-guess placement is unreliable, absent when a single dominant site exists, and occasionally negative when guardrails over-constrain a lucky guess.

Aggregate summaries (Table~\ref{tab:ocd62-tier1-full}) show the ablated variants at near parity with \textit{Full} (mean $|\Delta E| < 0.04$~eV), consistent with CMU20.
At the case level, however, ablated variants occasionally outperform Full: on case~007, \textit{w/o Slip} reaches $-19.925$~eV vs.\ \textit{Full} $-19.717$~eV; on case~008, Full is the worst of all five variants; on case~049, w/o Slip ($-8.628$~eV) outperforms Full ($-8.249$~eV).
These cases share a mechanism---when the first guess is already near-optimal, \texttt{FORBID} over-prunes---illustrating the reliability--exploration trade-off inherent to guardrail-based search.
Full also reduces the mean four-backend energy range to 0.183~eV from 0.316~eV under 1-Shot (1.73$\times$ reduction), a smaller compression than the 2.42$\times$ observed on CMU20, reflecting the greater difficulty of suppressing backend-specific biases on complex oxide and multi-element surfaces.

Under a fixed force field, energy depth is determined by sampling breadth, not reasoning strategy; AdsMind occupies the reliability-first, low-relaxation-budget corner of this trade-off space.

\subsubsection{Tier~2: Stability audit}

Hosted LLM services are black-box stochastic systems; single-run energies risk reflecting lucky initialization rather than reproducible performance.
Tier~2 quantifies this by repeating 12 cases in OCD-GMAE62 with $N=3$ independent runs. The distribution of their standard deviations are drawn in Figure~\ref{fig:ocd62-two-tier}b.
Across 240 valid comparisons, 176 (73.3\%) agree within 0.01~eV and 165 (68.8\%) within 0.001~eV.
For the Full variant: 38/48 (79.2\%) within 0.01~eV (72.9\% within 0.001~eV), median run-to-run range $<$0.001~eV, mean range 0.034~eV.
The dominant pattern is exact reproducibility: 72.9\% of Full runs converge to identical energies across independent trials, reflecting the closed-loop architecture's ability to drive heterogeneous LLM planners toward consistent physical solutions.
A thin stochastic tail ($\sim$21\%) exhibits genuine multi-basin exploration driven by LLM nondeterminism, with energy differences up to 0.5--1.0~eV; this residual variance means single-run energies should not be over-interpreted even under the min-var selection protocol.

Stability is architecture-dependent, not backend-dependent.
Ranking variants by median run-to-run range yields: Full $\approx$ w/o Slip ($<$0.001~eV) $<$ w/o Forbid $\approx$ w/o Term ($<$0.01~eV) $<$ 1-Shot (0.015~eV).
Removing individual mechanisms modestly increases variance (they are partially redundant suppressors), while removing all feedback increases the median range by over an order of magnitude.
This ranking holds across all four backends, confirming that iterative feedback---not LLM choice---is the primary driver of reproducibility.

Synthesizing across Tier~1 and Tier~2, OCD-GMAE62 defines the conditions under which AdsMind's closed-loop architecture adds value.
On complex surfaces with multi-atom adsorbates, the closed loop provides both reliability (98.8\% vs.\ 89.5\%) and energy improvement (mean $+0.329$~eV) with a mean budget of 4.67 MLFF relaxations per case.
On simple surfaces with a single dominant site, 1-Shot matches Full and iteration adds nothing beyond reliability guarantees.
On rare edge cases (case~008), guardrails over-constrain a lucky first guess, making Full worse by up to $\sim$1~eV.
For autonomous workflows that demand a high success rate and a tight relaxation budget, the 98.8\% success rate and $\sim$4-relaxation budget are decisive; for applications tolerating 10\% failure and 20--100 relaxations per case, brute-force enumeration will find deeper energies.
The closed-loop architecture is a purpose-built tool for the reliability-first corner of this trade-off space---not a universal replacement for exhaustive search.
